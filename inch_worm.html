Inch Worm Robot

Fabrication • Soft Robotics

Project Overview

This project focused on developing a bio-inspired soft robotic system using compliant materials to achieve controlled locomotion and object manipulation. Drawing inspiration from the inchworm's distinctive movement pattern, our team designed a multi-actuator system integrating accordion-style and angular pouch motors with deployable friction elements. The robot combines fundamental soft robotics principles with innovative actuation mechanisms to demonstrate adaptive movement, passive steering capabilities, and integrated grasping functionality. This project explores the intersection of biomimetic design and compliant mechanism engineering within the field of soft robotics.

Step 1: Initial Prototype Development

The first iteration of our soft robotic system employed an accordion motor as the primary actuator for axial extension and contraction, paired with inline pouch motors designed to deploy and retract silicone-based friction pads. This configuration was intended to replicate the alternating grip-and-release locomotion characteristic of inchworm movement.

Through experimental testing, we identified a critical limitation in the inline pouch motor design: the actuators generated insufficient normal force against the substrate surface, resulting in inadequate frictional coupling. This mechanical deficiency prevented effective anchoring during the extension phase, significantly limiting the robot's propulsive efficiency and overall displacement capabilities. These findings informed the mechanical redesign implemented in subsequent iterations.

Step 2: Second Prototype - Angular Pouch Motor Integration

To address the force application limitations observed in the initial prototype, we developed an angular pouch motor assembly incorporating a custom 3D-printed PLA support structure. This angled configuration enabled the pouch motors to generate directed normal forces against the substrate through the silicone friction pads, substantially increasing the static friction coefficient at the contact interface.

The angular geometry of the support structure was optimized to balance force vectoring with the available pneumatic pressure, resulting in significantly improved anchoring performance. Load testing demonstrated that the modified design could support the axial loads generated during the accordion motor's extension cycle, enabling controlled forward locomotion. This mechanical enhancement proved essential for achieving reliable inchworm-style movement and served as the foundation for subsequent design refinements.

Step 3: Final Design Integration

The final system architecture incorporated three major functional subsystems: the optimized angular pouch motor assemblies for traction control, an integrated pneumatic grasping mechanism for object manipulation, and passive mechanical steering elements. The passive steering feature enables autonomous trajectory correction when the robot encounters environmental obstacles, utilizing compliant mechanical responses rather than active control systems.

This multi-functional design demonstrates the capability of soft robotic systems to combine locomotion, manipulation, and adaptive navigation within a single integrated platform. The passive steering mechanism represents a particularly efficient approach to environmental interaction, reducing control system complexity while maintaining functional adaptability. Together, these systems showcase the potential of compliant mechanisms in creating versatile robotic platforms for constrained or unstructured environments.

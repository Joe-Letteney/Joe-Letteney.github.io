Gasoline Fuel Pump

Mechanical Design • Design for Manufacturing • GD&T • Project Documentation

Project Overview

This project built on prior experience manufacturing a rotary vane pump and focused on designing a new pump for a specific real-world application. The goal was to address known design shortcomings while meeting strict performance, reliability, and manufacturing requirements.

The design was developed to meet the following specifications:

  • Working fluid: Gasoline for a fuel injection system
  • Operating temperature: −40°F to 140°F
  • Inlet pressure: 4 bar
  • Outlet pressure: 300 bar (4351 psi)
  • Operating speed: 2000 rpm
  • Service life: 200,000 miles (MTBF)
  • Application: Full-size pickup truck
  • Annual production volume: 2,000,000 units

Phase 1: Engineering Analysis

The first phase of the project involved performing detailed engineering calculations to understand the forces and operating conditions each component would experience. This included evaluating PV values at the vane tips, estimating wear rates for both the vanes and housing, analyzing internal pressure loads on the housing and front plate, determining required bolt clamping forces, assessing tolerance stack-ups, and accounting for thermal expansion effects. These analyses established a clear set of performance and durability requirements that guided the final design. The complete calculation package is provided below.

Phase 2: Material Selection

Material selection focused on balancing strength, cost, and manufacturability for high-pressure, high-volume production. Given the operating pressures and annual production volume, the selected material needed to offer high tensile strength at a low cost and be compatible with sand casting to reduce manufacturing expense. Using Granta EduPack, multiple candidate materials were evaluated against these criteria, leading to the selection of gray cast iron for its favorable combination of strength, cost, and castability.

Phase 3: Design Challenges and Solutions

Engineering analysis revealed extremely high PV values at the vane tips, eliminating polymer materials and necessitating the use of hardened tool steel. To reduce the resulting outward forces on the vanes, a revised vane geometry was developed that allows controlled fluid circulation while maintaining an effective seal. This approach significantly balanced pressure across the vane surfaces, leaving centrifugal force as the primary outward load. To address wear concerns from steel-on-steel contact, precision-machined PEEK inserts were added to the housing. These inserts greatly reduced wear rates and enabled the design to meet its required service life. In addition, inlet and outlet port geometry was optimized to prevent volume changes between vane chambers, minimizing the risk of cavitation.

Phase 4: Design for Manufacturing

Manufacturing considerations were central to the design due to the high annual production volume. The main housing was designed for sand casting using a single-core configuration to reduce tooling complexity and cost. The PEEK inserts were optimized for injection molding to ensure consistent quality and dimensional control at scale. The vanes were designed for powder metal sintering, providing excellent dimensional accuracy and compatibility with hardened tool steel materials.

Phase 5: Technical Documentation

The project concluded with the creation of a complete, manufacturing-ready technical drawing package. This included detailed part drawings with full GD&T, casting drawings with pattern and core specifications, and injection molding drawings for polymer inserts. Together, these documents provide a comprehensive reference suitable for direct production implementation.

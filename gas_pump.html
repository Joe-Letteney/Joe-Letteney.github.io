Gasoline Fuel Pump

Mechanical Design • Design For Manufacturing • GD&T • Project Documentation

Project Overview

The goal of this project was to build on the experience manufacturing a rotary vane pump to design a pump for a specific application while fixing many of the flaws that we had discovered in the original design. Our teams specifications were as follows:

Substance: Gasoline for Fuel Injection System

  • Temperature: -40F to 140F
  • Inlet Pressure: 4 bar
  • Outlet Pressure: 300 bar (4351psi)
  • Speed: 2000 rpm
  • MTBF: 200,000 miles of vehicle travel
  • Installation: Large pickup truck
  • Yearly: Qty 2,000,000 units per year

Phase 1: Engineering Analysis

The first phase of this project was to preform in depth calculations as to what forces and conditions our parts would have to be able to withstand based on our operating conditions. These calculate the PV value at the tip of the vanes, the wear rate on both the vanes and the hosuing, the pressure the hosuing and front plate would have to withsand, the clamping force and number of bolts required, tolerance stackup, and thermal expansion. These calculations gave us a foundation of requirements that our desing needed to meet. You can read our entire calculation document below.

Phase 2: Material Selection

The next phase of this porject was to choose what materials to make the pump out of. Due to the high pressures and very high yearly production quantity we need a material with high tensile stength, low cost, and the ability to be sadn cast to further drive down manufacturing costs. Using Granta Edupack we set up several searchs based on these criteria everntually deciding on cast iron gray for its blend of low cost and strength.

Phase 3: Design Challenges and Solutions

Analysis revealed an exceptionally high PV value at the vane tip, necessitating elimination of all polymer options in favor of hardened tool steel. To mitigate the resulting high outward forces on the vanes, an innovative vane geometry was developed that permits fluid circulation while maintaining sealing integrity. This design achieves near-perfect pressure balance across the vane surfaces, with centrifugal force becoming the dominant outward load component. The steel-on-steel interface between vanes and housing presented significant wear concerns, which were addressed through the integration of precision-machined PEEK inserts. These inserts substantially reduce the wear rate, enabling the pump to achieve its specified service life. Also to aviod cavitation, the inlet and outlet ports were designed so that volume of the fluid contained betweeen tow sets of vanes never changes.

Phase 4: Design for Manufacturing

Manufacturing cost optimization for high-volume production was achieved through strategic process selection. The main housing was designed for sand casting with a single-core configuration, significantly reducing tooling complexity and per-unit cost. Concurrently, the PEEK insert geometry was optimized for injection molding, ensuring consistent quality and dimensional accuracy while maintaining manufacturing efficiency at scale. Finally the vanes are manufactured using powdered metal sinitering due to its high dimensional accuracy and compatibility with tool steel.

Phase 5: Technical Documentation

The project culminated in the development of a complete technical drawing package. This documentation suite includes detailed part drawings with comprehensive GD&T specifications, casting drawings for all cast components with complete pattern and core specifications, and injection molding drawings for polymer inserts with full process parameters. The drawing set provides manufacturing-ready documentation suitable for immediate production implementation.

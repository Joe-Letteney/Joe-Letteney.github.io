Automatic Pipetter for 96-Well PCR

Automation • Motion Control • Laboratory Equipment

Project Overview

The goal of this project was to design a device with three degrees of freedom that would solve a real-world laboratory challenge. Our team developed an automatic pipetter capable of dispensing liquid quickly and accurately into a 96-well PCR plate. Manual pipetting is time-consuming and tedious, making it an ideal candidate for automation. This device provides an efficient and cost-effective solution to streamline laboratory workflows.

Step 1: Single Linear Stage

The first step involved creating a linear stage capable of moving a small cart in one dimension. We constructed the frame from foam board and integrated a stepper motor with a lead screw to achieve precise linear motion. This prototype served as the foundation for understanding motion control and mechanical constraints.

Step 2: Three-Axis Motion System

The next phase required integrating three linear stages to achieve full three-dimensional movement. We collaborated with two other teams to stack one linear stage on top of two others, adding a third stepper motor to control vertical movement. To validate the system, we programmed it to draw shapes on paper using G-code commands. This exercise provided crucial experience with multi-axis coordination and motion control.

Step 3: Mechanical Design

With a solid understanding of stepper motors and G-code from the drawing device, we began designing the automatic pipetter system. While my teammate focused on optimizing the linear stages, I took the lead on designing the pipette mounting mechanism. We selected a Femto Scientific P10 pipette for this application.

I designed a custom mounting plate that securely holds the pipette while allowing controlled vertical movement. The design incorporates a rack and pinion mechanism at the top to actuate the pipette button with precision. An additional rack mounted to the back of the plate interfaces with another stepper motor to control the vertical axis. Below are the SolidWorks assembly views showing the complete mechanism.

Step 4: Automation Software

Automating the pipetting process required generating precise G-code commands for each well in the 96-well plate. Since each pipetting operation requires 13 unique motor commands, manually writing code for all wells would be impractical and error-prone. To solve this, I developed a Python script that automatically generates all necessary commands and exports them to a G-code file.

We used Repetier-Host, a 3D printer control software, to execute the G-code and operate the device. The Python script proved invaluable as it allowed us to quickly iterate on motion parameters and optimize the thousands of commands required for complete plate coverage.

Step 5: Testing and Optimization

During initial testing, we discovered that the original stepper motors lacked sufficient torque to move the pipette vertically as designed. We first attempted to resolve this by adjusting gear ratios to maximize available torque. However, geometric constraints in the system limited the effectiveness of this approach.

Our solution was to replace the large stepper motor with a smaller toy motor and reduce the vertical movement speed, increasing contact time and effective torque. These modifications successfully achieved the required motion. We then fine-tuned the system by precisely locating each well position and optimizing step sizes for accurate pipetting across the entire plate.

Step 6: Final Presentation

After completing testing and optimization, we presented our automated pipetting system to our professor and fellow students, demonstrating the complete functionality and discussing the engineering challenges we overcame during development.

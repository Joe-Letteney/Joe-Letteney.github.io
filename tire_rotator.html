Tire Rotator

Mechanical Design • GD&T • Project Documentation

Project Overview

The goal of this project was to design a device that addresses a common problem associated with long-term vehicle storage: tire flat spotting. When a car sits unused for extended periods, the portion of each tire in constant contact with the ground can deform and thin, creating flat spots that compromise ride quality, safety, and often require premature tire replacement.


Existing solutions typically rely on static tire cradles that distribute vehicle weight over a larger surface area. While helpful, these solutions do not fully eliminate flat spotting and are often cumbersome to install and remove, particularly for heavier or high-value vehicles.


Our solution reimagines the traditional car dolly by integrating a controlled tire-rotation mechanism. By periodically rotating each tire during storage, the system actively prevents flat spot formation rather than merely mitigating it. Because the design is based on a car jack dolly, it is quick to deploy, easy to remove, and provides the added benefit of allowing vehicles to be repositioned effortlessly in tight spaces—without starting the engine.

Step 1: Market Research

The first phase of this project focused on market research to identify the features most critical to the product’s success. We evaluated existing flat-spot prevention and vehicle mobility solutions to understand their limitations, and surveyed over 40 private collectors and professional storage facilities to uncover real-world pain points.


This research revealed two key insights: users place a high value on being able to reposition vehicles easily without starting the engine, and current flat-spot mitigation solutions do not adequately solve the problem. These findings directly informed the direction of our concept and feature set.


Based on this research, we developed both a Market Requirements Document (MRD) and a Product Requirements Document (PRD). These documents established clear user needs, technical constraints, and success criteria, and served as the foundation for all subsequent design decisions. Step 2: Testing

One of the most critical technical requirements of our product was ensuring that the motors could generate enough torque to rotate the vehicle’s wheels under load. To quantify this requirement, we conducted a hands-on experiment to estimate the resistance the system would need to overcome.


A vehicle was placed in neutral on level ground to eliminate drivetrain locking effects. To prevent unintended movement, the brakes were released only after confirming the car would not roll freely. A bathroom scale was then positioned against the rear bumper, and force was applied gradually until the vehicle began to move. The measured force provided an estimate of the vehicle’s rolling resistance and internal friction, which informed the minimum torque requirements for motor selection and drivetrain design.

Step 3: Design Overview

Our system is made up of four modified car dolly jacks, each connected to a central electrical control box. A jack is positioned at each wheel and raised just enough so the tire lifts slightly off the ground and rests on two roller elements built into the dolly. Once the vehicle is in neutral, the jacks are connected to the control box, which periodically powers the system to rotate each wheel by a small amount. This controlled rotation prevents flat spotting during long-term storage.


Because each dolly is mounted on caster wheels, the vehicle can also be easily moved in any direction without starting the engine, making it ideal for tight storage spaces or showroom layouts.


Each jack includes both an active and a passive roller. The passive roller is a freely rotating tube that supports the tire, while the active roller houses two planetary gear motors that provide the torque needed to rotate the wheel under load.
